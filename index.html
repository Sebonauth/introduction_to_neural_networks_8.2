At its core, a neural network processes inputs to produce an output. This transformation happens through a series of mathematical operations at each neuron.

Continue

Imagine you're trying to predict the price of a house based on its size, number of bedrooms, and age. Each of these features is an input to our network.

Continue

These inputs are combined using weights and biases to compute a weighted sum, often referred to as \( z \).

Build Your Vocab

Weighted Sum (\( z \)): The weighted sum is the sum of each input multiplied by its corresponding weight, plus a bias term. It represents the net input to a neuron before the activation function is applied.

Continue

Mathematically, for a single neuron, this is expressed as:

\[ z = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \dots + w_n x_n + b \]

Continue

Here, \( x_i \) are the inputs, \( w_i \) are the weights, and \( b \) is the bias.

Continue

Once we've calculated \( z \), we apply an activation function to introduce non-linearity into the model.

Build Your Vocab

Activation Function: An activation function is applied to the weighted sum \( z \) to produce the neuron's output \( a \). It allows the network to capture non-linear relationships.

Continue

The output of the neuron is then:

\[ a = \sigma(z) \]

Continue

Where \( \sigma \) represents the activation function, such as sigmoid, ReLU, or tanh.

If a neuron has inputs \( x_1 = 2 \), \( x_2 = -1 \), weights \( w_1 = 0.5 \), \( w_2 = 1 \), and bias \( b = -0.5 \), what is the weighted sum \( z \)?

Correct! \( z = (0.5)(2) + (1)(-1) + (-0.5) = -0.5 \).

Now, applying the activation function to \( z = -0.5 \), we get the neuron's output \( a \). For example, using the sigmoid activation function:

\[ a = \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} \]

Continue

Plugging in \( z = -0.5 \):

\[ a = \frac{1}{1 + e^{0.5}} \approx \frac{1}{1 + 1.6487} \approx 0.3775 \]

Continue

So, the neuron's output is approximately 0.3775.
